The Rhyme

Solomon Grundy

Born on Monday

Christened on Tuesday

Married on Wednesday

Took ill on Thursday

Worse on Friday

Died on Saturday

Buried on Sunday

This is the end of Solomon Grundy

The Story behind the Rhyme

In the quiet corners of nursery rhyme history, there exists a character whose life unfolds not in years, but in a single, fleeting week. His name is Solomon Grundy, and his story is one of the most compressed — and strangely poignant — biographies in the English language.

First recorded in the 19th century by nursery rhyme collector James Orchard Halliwell-Phillipps, the rhyme offers a complete human arc from birth to burial in just seven days. No childhood, no middle age — just the milestones: birth, faith, marriage, sickness, death, and burial.

Meaning and Interpretation

Some literary scholars interpret Solomon Grundy as a reminder of life's brevity. Others see him as a darkly comic figure whose hurried existence reflects Victorian fascination with time and mortality. The name itself carries weight: "Solomon" suggests wisdom, while "Grundy" hints at "everyman." He is no hero or king — just a man whose week becomes his lifetime.

Despite its grim conclusion, the rhyme has endured for generations. Children chant it without grasping its melancholy; adults hear it and pause. Solomon Grundy reminds us that life, however long it feels, can be summarized in a few short lines. Born, christened, married, sick, dead, buried. That was the end.

And yet, every time the rhyme is spoken, Solomon Grundy begins again — born on a Monday, as if for the very first time.
